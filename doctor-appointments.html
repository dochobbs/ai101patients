Preparing for Doctor Appointments

Use AI to organize your thoughts, prepare questions, and make the most of limited time with your doctor.

18 min read

Why Preparation Matters

Doctor appointments are short. The average primary care visit is about 15-20 minutes, and that includes time for the doctor to review your chart, examine you, and document the visit. Your actual conversation time might be 8-10 minutes.

If you walk in unprepared, it's easy to forget important symptoms, ramble about things that aren't priorities, or leave without asking your most important questions. AI can help you prepare so you make the most of every minute.

The benefits of preparation extend beyond just time efficiency. When you come prepared, you're a better advocate for your own health. You can communicate more clearly, ask better questions, and leave with a clearer understanding of your care plan.

Pearl

Research shows that patients who prepare questions before appointments report higher satisfaction and better understanding of their care plan. AI makes this preparation faster and more thorough.

Source: Studies on patient preparation have been published in journals including Patient Education and Counseling and the Journal of General Internal Medicine.

Learn More: The research on appointment preparation

Multiple studies have examined how patient preparation affects healthcare encounters:

Question asking: Patients who prepare questions in advance ask more questions during visits and are more likely to get their concerns addressed.

Recall: Preparation improves how much patients remember from their visits, leading to better adherence to treatment plans.

Satisfaction: Prepared patients report higher satisfaction with their care and their doctor-patient relationship.

Health outcomes: Some studies suggest that better-prepared patients have improved health outcomes, though this is harder to measure directly.

AI tools make it easier than ever to prepare effectively, lowering the barrier to getting these benefits.

Step 1: Organize Your Symptoms

Before your appointment, use AI to help you create a clear, organized summary of what's been going on. This saves time and helps your doctor understand your situation quickly.

Prompt to try:

I have an appointment with my doctor about [main concern]. Help me organize the following symptoms into a clear summary: [List your symptoms, when they started, how often they happen, what makes them better or worse, etc.] Please format this as a brief summary I can hand to my doctor or read quickly at the start of my appointment.

What to Include

  • Timeline: When did this start? Has it changed over time?
  • Frequency: How often does it happen? Constant? Occasional?
  • Severity: On a 1-10 scale, how bad is it at worst? On average?
  • Triggers: Does anything make it worse? Better?
  • Impact: How is this affecting your daily life?
  • What you've tried: Any treatments, medications, or home remedies?
  • Associated symptoms: What else happens along with the main symptom?
  • Relevant history: Previous similar issues, family history if relevant

The OLDCARTS Framework

Doctors often use a framework called OLDCARTS to understand symptoms. If you organize your information this way, you're speaking their language:

  • Onset: When did it start?
  • Location: Where exactly is it?
  • Duration: How long does each episode last?
  • Character: What does it feel like? (Sharp, dull, throbbing, etc.)
  • Alleviating/Aggravating: What makes it better or worse?
  • Radiation: Does it spread to other areas?
  • Timing: Is there a pattern? (Morning, after eating, etc.)
  • Severity: How bad is it on a 1-10 scale?
Try This

Before your next appointment, spend 5 minutes describing your symptoms to AI in your own words. Then ask it to organize what you said into a clear, one-paragraph summary using the OLDCARTS framework. Bring that summary to your appointment.

Learn More: Creating a symptom diary with AI

For ongoing or recurring symptoms, keeping a diary can be incredibly valuable. AI can help you create and summarize one:

Daily tracking: Each day, tell AI about your symptoms. It can help you identify patterns over time.

Pre-appointment summary: Before your appointment, ask AI to summarize your diary entries: "Based on what I've told you over the past two weeks, summarize the patterns in my symptoms."

Correlation finding: AI can help identify correlations you might miss: "Do my headaches seem to correlate with anything else I've mentioned—sleep, stress, food, weather?"

This kind of systematic tracking can provide your doctor with much richer information than you could recall from memory.

Step 2: Prepare Your Questions

You always have questions for your doctor. But under the pressure of a short appointment, it's easy to forget them or not know how to phrase them. AI can help you prepare a prioritized list.

Prompt to try:

I'm seeing my [type of doctor] about [reason for visit]. My background: [brief relevant history - age, conditions, medications] Help me create a list of 5-7 questions I should consider asking. Focus on: - Understanding my condition or symptoms - Treatment options and what to expect - Warning signs I should watch for - Lifestyle changes that might help Put the most important questions first.

Types of Questions Worth Asking

  • Understanding: "Can you explain what this means in simple terms?"
  • Options: "What are my treatment options, and what do you recommend?"
  • Next steps: "What should I do next? When should I follow up?"
  • Warning signs: "What symptoms should make me call you or go to the ER?"
  • Lifestyle: "Are there lifestyle changes that would help with this?"
  • Medications: "What side effects should I watch for? How long should I take this?"
  • Prognosis: "What can I expect over time? Will this get better, stay the same, or get worse?"
  • Second opinion: "Is this something where a second opinion might be helpful?"

Prioritizing Your Questions

You may not have time for all your questions. Prioritize by asking:

  • What do I absolutely need to know before I leave?
  • What would affect my immediate decisions or safety?
  • What questions could wait for a follow-up or portal message?
Pearl

Bring your question list on paper or your phone. If you run out of time, ask your doctor: "I have a few more questions - should I schedule another appointment, or can I send them through the patient portal?"

Step 3: Understand Before You Go

If you're seeing a specialist or discussing a specific condition, AI can help you understand the basics before your appointment. This lets you have a more informed conversation.

Prompt to try:

I'm seeing a [specialist type] for the first time because [reason]. Please give me a brief overview of: 1. What this type of doctor specializes in 2. What I should expect during the visit 3. Basic terms related to [condition] I might hear 4. What information or records I should bring Keep it simple and focused on what will help me prepare.

Building a Vocabulary

Understanding basic medical terms helps you follow the conversation and ask better questions. Before a specialist appointment, ask AI:

  • "What are the key medical terms I might hear related to [condition]?"
  • "Explain these terms in simple language: [list of terms from your records]"
  • "What tests might my doctor order, and what do they check for?"
In the News

Research Finding: Studies show that patients who understand their conditions better are more likely to follow treatment plans and have better health outcomes. Preparation isn't just about efficiency - it leads to better care.

Source: Health literacy research has consistently shown correlations between patient understanding and treatment adherence.

Learn More: Preparing for specific types of appointments

Different types of appointments benefit from different preparation:

Annual physical: Review any symptoms you've had since your last visit, update your medication list, think about preventive care questions.

Specialist consult: Bring relevant records and test results, understand why you were referred, prepare questions about next steps.

Follow-up visit: Track how you've been since the last visit, note any medication side effects, prepare questions about your progress.

New symptom visit: Focus on OLDCARTS (see above), bring any relevant photos or documentation, think about timeline.

Results review: Prepare questions about what the results mean, what the next steps are, and what changes you need to make.

What to Bring vs. What to Verify

AI can help you prepare materials to bring to your appointment. But some things AI produces should be verified rather than handed directly to your doctor.

Good to Bring

  • Your symptom summary (organized by AI)
  • Your question list (generated with AI help)
  • Timeline of when symptoms started
  • List of current medications and supplements
  • Notes on what you've already tried
  • Your goals for the visit
  • Photos of visible symptoms (rashes, swelling)
  • Records from other providers if relevant

Verify First

  • Any "diagnosis" AI suggested - bring as a question
  • Statistics or research AI cited - may be made up
  • Treatment recommendations - let your doctor decide
  • Drug interaction warnings - pharmacist should verify
  • Claims that contradict previous medical advice
  • Dosage information - always verify with provider

Creating a One-Page Summary

For complex situations or new doctors, ask AI to help you create a one-page health summary:

Prompt to try:

Help me create a one-page health summary for a new doctor. Include: - My basic info: [age, sex] - Current conditions: [list your diagnoses] - Current medications: [list your medications with doses] - Allergies: [list allergies] - Major surgeries/hospitalizations: [list if any] - Family history highlights: [relevant family history] - Current concern: [why you're seeing this doctor] Format this clearly so a doctor can scan it quickly.
Warning

Don't tell your doctor "AI diagnosed me with X." Instead say: "I was reading about my symptoms and X came up. Is that something we should consider?" This respects your doctor's expertise while still raising the topic.

During and After the Appointment

During Your Appointment

  • Lead with your main concern: "The most important thing I want to discuss today is..."
  • Use your prepared summary to explain symptoms clearly and efficiently
  • Take notes, or ask if you can record the conversation
  • Ask for clarification: "Can you explain that in simpler terms?"
  • Before you leave, confirm: "So the next steps are..." to make sure you understand
  • Ask: "What should I call you about vs. what can wait until my follow-up?"
  • If you didn't get to all your questions, ask how to follow up

Recording Your Appointment

Many patients find it helpful to record appointments (with permission) so they can review the information later. If you do:

  • Always ask your doctor first
  • Use your phone's voice recorder app
  • After the appointment, AI can help you summarize the recording
  • Focus on understanding, not having a "record" of what was said

After Your Appointment

AI can help you make sense of what happened during your appointment:

Prompt to try:

I just had a doctor appointment about [reason]. Here's what my doctor told me: [Summarize what you remember - diagnosis, treatment plan, follow-up instructions] Can you help me: 1. Understand any medical terms they used 2. Organize the next steps I need to take 3. Identify anything I should ask about if I'm unclear

Creating an Action List

After your appointment, use AI to turn what you learned into actionable steps:

Prompt to try:

Based on my doctor visit, here's what I need to do: [List what the doctor recommended] Help me create a clear action list with: 1. Things to do immediately 2. Things to do this week 3. Things to remember for the long term 4. Warning signs to watch for Include any reminders about medications, follow-up appointments, or lifestyle changes.
Try This

After your next appointment, write down everything you remember within an hour. Then ask AI to help you organize it. If there are gaps or confusion, use your patient portal to send a follow-up question to your doctor's office.

Learn More: Using AI with your patient portal messages

Many patients now use their patient portal to send messages to their healthcare team. AI can help you write clearer, more effective messages.

Try: "Help me write a clear, concise message to my doctor about [concern]. Include relevant background and specific questions."

This helps you communicate effectively without writing an essay or forgetting important details. Keep messages focused - your doctor reads many messages a day.

Tips for portal messages:

  • Be specific about what you're asking
  • Include relevant context (when symptoms started, what you've tried)
  • Keep it concise - aim for 1-2 short paragraphs
  • Ask one or two questions, not many
  • Indicate urgency if appropriate
Learn More: Following up effectively

Good follow-up is an important part of your care. AI can help you:

Track your progress: If you were given a treatment plan, document how it's going so you can report back.

Notice changes: Keep track of any new symptoms or changes that develop after your appointment.

Prepare for follow-ups: Before your next appointment, review what was discussed last time and prepare to update your doctor.

Know when to reach out: If you experience warning signs your doctor mentioned, or if something seems significantly wrong, don't wait for your follow-up.

Special Situations

Preparing for a Second Opinion

Prompt to try:

I'm getting a second opinion about [condition/diagnosis/recommended treatment]. My current situation: [brief background] My current doctor recommends: [current recommendation] My concerns are: [your concerns] Help me prepare questions to ask the new doctor. I want to understand if there are alternative approaches without undermining my current doctor.

Advocating for Yourself or a Family Member

Prompt to try:

I'm accompanying [relationship] to a doctor appointment about [concern]. They have [relevant history]. I'm worried the doctor might dismiss their concerns because [reason - e.g., previous experience, communication challenges]. Help me prepare: 1. How to clearly present their symptoms 2. Questions to ensure thorough evaluation 3. Phrases to advocate for them respectfully but firmly

Navigating Complex or Serious Diagnoses

Prompt to try:

I've been diagnosed with [serious condition] and have an appointment to discuss treatment options. Help me prepare: 1. Questions about the diagnosis and what it means 2. Questions about treatment options and their pros/cons 3. Questions about prognosis and what to expect 4. Questions about support resources I want to be informed and prepared without being overwhelmed.

Telemedicine Appointments

Video visits require some additional preparation:

  • Test your technology beforehand
  • Find a quiet, well-lit space
  • Have your vitals ready if you can measure them (blood pressure, temperature, weight)
  • Have your medication bottles nearby so you can show them if needed
  • Prepare to describe symptoms you can't show on camera

Key Takeaways

  • Use AI to organize your symptoms into a clear, concise summary before appointments
  • Prepare a prioritized list of questions - you may only have time for a few
  • Use the OLDCARTS framework to describe symptoms the way doctors think
  • Research your condition basics so you can have an informed conversation
  • Bring AI-organized materials, but verify AI-generated claims before presenting them as fact
  • Create a one-page health summary for new doctors or complex situations
  • After appointments, use AI to help understand and organize what you learned
  • Lead with your main concern - don't save the most important thing for last
  • Use your patient portal for follow-up questions, with AI helping you write clear messages
