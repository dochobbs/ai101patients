How to Talk to AI About Your Health

Simple techniques to get better, more useful answers from AI chatbots.

18 min read

Why How You Ask Matters

AI chatbots respond differently depending on how you ask your question. A vague question gets a vague answer. A specific, well-structured question gets something much more useful.

This isn't about learning special tricks or secret commands. It's about the same skills you use when talking to anyone: being clear, giving context, and asking for what you actually need.

The way you communicate with AI is sometimes called "prompting." While there's a whole industry around "prompt engineering," you don't need to be an expert. The basic principles are intuitive once you understand how AI works.

Pearl

Think of AI like a very knowledgeable friend who just met you. They want to help, but they only know what you tell them. The more context you provide, the better help they can give.

Learn More: Why AI responds differently to different prompts

AI chatbots work by predicting what response would be most helpful given your input. When you provide more specific, detailed information, the AI has more to work with in crafting its response.

Think of it like asking directions. "How do I get there?" requires guessing where "there" is and where you're starting from. "How do I get from downtown Chicago to O'Hare Airport using public transit?" gives everything needed for a useful answer.

The same principle applies to health questions. "Why am I tired?" could have thousands of answers. "I'm a 45-year-old woman who sleeps 7 hours but wakes up tired, especially on days after I eat late. What might explain this?" narrows it down significantly.

The Four Keys to Better Questions

1. Be Specific

Vague questions lead to generic answers. The more specific you are, the more tailored and useful the response will be.

Instead of this:

What causes headaches?

Try this:

I get headaches that start behind my right eye, usually in the afternoon, about 3-4 times a week. They've been happening for the past month. What might be causing this pattern?

The second question will get you information specific to your situation rather than a generic list of headache causes.

2. Provide Context

AI doesn't know your medical history unless you tell it. Including relevant background helps get more accurate, personalized responses.

Adding helpful context:

I'm a 45-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes. I take metformin. My doctor wants to add a new medication called Jardiance. Can you explain what Jardiance does and how it might work together with metformin?

Context that can be helpful includes:

  • Your age and sex
  • Relevant medical conditions
  • Current medications
  • Family history (if relevant)
  • Lifestyle factors (activity level, diet, sleep patterns)
  • What treatments you've already tried
Pearl

If you've connected ChatGPT Health to your medical records, you can say things like "Based on my health records, what questions should I ask about..." The AI will use your actual data to give more personalized suggestions.

3. Ask for the Format You Need

You can ask AI to present information in different ways: simple language, bullet points, comparison tables, or step-by-step explanations.

Specifying format:

Explain the difference between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. Use simple language that someone without a medical background could understand. Include a comparison table.

Another format example:

Give me a bullet-point list of questions I should ask my cardiologist about my high blood pressure. Keep it to the 5 most important questions.

Format options to consider:

  • Simple language: "Explain this like I'm explaining to a family member"
  • Bullet points: Easier to scan and remember
  • Tables: Great for comparing options
  • Step-by-step: Helpful for procedures or processes
  • Summary: When you want the key points only
  • Detailed: When you want comprehensive information

4. Tell It Your Goal

Explain why you're asking. This helps the AI focus on what's actually useful to you.

Including your goal:

I was just diagnosed with hypothyroidism and I'm feeling overwhelmed. I want to understand the basics before my follow-up appointment next week. Can you explain what hypothyroidism means for my daily life in a way that won't scare me?

Stating your goal helps in several ways:

  • The AI adjusts its tone (reassuring vs. clinical)
  • It focuses on actionable information
  • It avoids overwhelming you with irrelevant details
  • It can structure information to meet your needs
Try This

Take a health question you've been curious about. Before asking it, add: (1) relevant personal context, (2) your format preference, and (3) why you're asking. Notice how the answer changes compared to a simple question.

Example Prompts That Work Well

Here are some proven prompt patterns for common health situations:

Understanding a New Diagnosis

Prompt template:

I was just diagnosed with [condition]. I'm feeling [emotional state]. Can you: 1. Explain what this condition means in simple terms 2. Tell me what I might expect in the short term 3. Suggest 3-5 questions I should ask my doctor Please use reassuring language and avoid medical jargon.

Understanding a Lab Result

Prompt template:

My [lab test] came back at [value]. The normal range is [range]. Can you explain: 1. What this test measures 2. What my result might indicate 3. What questions I should ask my doctor about this result I'm a [age]-year-old [male/female] with [any relevant conditions].

Comparing Treatment Options

Prompt template:

My doctor gave me a choice between [option A] and [option B] for [condition]. Can you help me understand: 1. How each option works 2. The main pros and cons of each 3. What questions I should ask to help me decide Format this as a comparison table if possible.

Preparing for an Appointment

Prompt template:

I have an appointment with my [type of doctor] in [timeframe] about [concern]. My relevant history: [brief background]. Help me: 1. Organize my symptoms and concerns clearly 2. Create a list of important questions to ask 3. Identify any information I should bring or prepare

Understanding Medication Side Effects

Prompt template:

I'm taking [medication] for [condition]. I'm experiencing [symptom/concern]. Can you help me understand: 1. Is this a known side effect of this medication? 2. Is it usually temporary or ongoing? 3. Should I contact my doctor about this, and if so, what should I tell them? Please differentiate between common/mild side effects and ones that need medical attention.
In the News

Research Finding: Studies show that patients who prepare questions before appointments report higher satisfaction and better understanding of their care plan. AI can be a helpful tool for this preparation.

Source: Research on patient question preparation has been published in medical journals including Patient Education and Counseling.

Learn More: Creating your own prompt templates

Once you find prompts that work well for you, save them for reuse. You can create templates for situations you encounter regularly:

For chronic conditions: Create a template that includes your condition, current medications, and typical concerns.

For recurring appointments: Create a pre-appointment template with your standard context already filled in.

For medication questions: Keep a template that asks about interactions, timing, and side effects.

You can save these in a notes app and copy-paste them when needed, filling in the specific details for each situation.

Following Up and Going Deeper

A conversation with AI is just that: a conversation. You don't have to get everything right in the first message. You can follow up, clarify, and dig deeper.

Useful Follow-up Phrases

  • "Can you explain that in simpler terms?"
  • "What do you mean by [specific term]?"
  • "Can you give me a specific example?"
  • "What would this look like in everyday life?"
  • "Is there anything you're uncertain about in your answer?"
  • "What are the most important things I should remember from this?"
  • "Can you summarize that in 2-3 sentences?"
  • "What else should I know that I haven't asked about?"

Probing for Uncertainty

AI often sounds more confident than it should be. You can ask it to acknowledge uncertainty directly:

Asking about confidence:

You sounded very confident in that answer. How sure are you about this information? What parts should I definitely verify with my doctor?
Pearl

Don't be afraid to ask AI to be more cautious: "You sounded very confident. How sure are you about this information, and what should I verify with my doctor?" This can help surface uncertainty that AI might otherwise gloss over.

When the Answer Doesn't Help

If you get an unhelpful response, try:

  • Rephrasing your question with more detail
  • Breaking a complex question into smaller parts
  • Explicitly asking for a different approach: "That was too technical. Can you explain it like I'm explaining to a family member?"
  • Starting a new conversation with fresh context
  • Asking a different AI tool (different tools have different strengths)

Building on Previous Answers

AI remembers what you've discussed in the same conversation. Use this to go deeper:

  • "Based on what we just discussed, what should I do next?"
  • "Given my situation, which of those options makes the most sense?"
  • "Can you relate that to what you said earlier about [topic]?"
  • "Now that you understand my background, what questions should I ask my doctor?"
Try This

After getting an answer, ask: "What's the most important thing I should take away from this, and what's the first thing I should verify with a healthcare professional?" This helps you prioritize and reminds you to verify.

Managing Sensitive Health Topics

Some health topics feel more sensitive or embarrassing to discuss. One advantage of AI is that you can ask about anything without judgment. However, there are still considerations:

Privacy Considerations

  • Remember that your conversations may be stored (unless you use temporary chat mode)
  • For very sensitive topics, consider using temporary/private chat modes
  • Don't include identifying information you don't need to share
  • Be aware of who might have access to your devices

Getting Useful Answers on Sensitive Topics

AI is often cautious about sensitive health topics. If you're getting overly generic responses, try being more specific about what you need:

Being specific about sensitive topics:

I'm experiencing [sensitive symptom]. I've been hesitant to bring this up with my doctor because [reason]. Can you: 1. Help me understand if this is something I should see a doctor about 2. Suggest how I might bring this up with my doctor 3. Explain what kind of doctor would be appropriate to see I'm looking for practical guidance, not just "see a doctor."
Learn More: Using AI for mental health topics

AI can be helpful for learning about mental health conditions, understanding treatment options, and preparing for appointments. However, it has limitations:

What AI can help with:

  • Explaining mental health conditions in accessible terms
  • Describing what therapy or treatment might involve
  • Helping you organize your thoughts before a mental health appointment
  • Providing coping strategies based on established techniques

What AI cannot do:

  • Provide therapy or mental health treatment
  • Diagnose mental health conditions
  • Replace ongoing care from a mental health professional
  • Provide crisis intervention

In crisis: If you're experiencing a mental health crisis, please contact a crisis line or emergency services. AI is not appropriate for crisis support.

What Not to Do

Some approaches will consistently give you worse results:

  • Don't ask yes/no questions about diagnoses: "Do I have cancer?" will get a generic response. "What could cause these symptoms?" starts a more useful conversation.
  • Don't expect mind-reading: AI doesn't know details you haven't shared. If context matters, include it.
  • Don't ignore uncertainty: If AI says "might," "could," or "possibly," those words matter.
  • Don't use AI for emergencies: Chest pain, difficulty breathing, or severe symptoms need 911, not a chatbot.
  • Don't skip verification: Always verify important information with your healthcare team.
  • Don't argue with AI: If you disagree with an answer, rephrase your question rather than arguing.
  • Don't share more than necessary: You can be specific without including identifying information you don't need to share.
Warning

Never tell AI to "ignore previous instructions" or try to "jailbreak" it for medical advice. Safety guardrails exist for good reasons. If AI won't give you specific medical advice, that's a feature, not a bug.

Common Mistakes That Lead to Poor Answers

  • Being too brief: "Should I worry?" gives AI nothing to work with
  • Being too lengthy: Very long messages may bury the actual question
  • Multiple unrelated questions: Ask one topic at a time for better focus
  • Leading questions: "Isn't it true that..." pushes AI toward what you want to hear
  • Expecting diagnosis: AI is trained not to diagnose, so don't expect it to
Learn More: Why AI has safety guardrails

AI companies build in safety features that prevent AI from giving direct medical diagnoses or treatment recommendations. You might find this frustrating if you want a straight answer, but there are good reasons for it.

AI can sound confident while being wrong. It doesn't know your full situation. It can't examine you or order tests. If it gave direct medical advice and that advice was wrong, people could be seriously harmed.

The guardrails are designed to keep AI in its lane: helping you understand information and prepare questions, not replacing professional medical judgment.

When AI says "consult a healthcare provider," it's not being unhelpful—it's recognizing the limits of what it can safely do.

Learn More: What to do when AI seems too cautious

Sometimes AI refuses to engage with questions that seem perfectly reasonable. This happens because the guardrails are intentionally broad to avoid edge cases.

If this happens, try:

Reframe as education: "I'm trying to understand [topic] in general terms" rather than "What should I do about..."

Ask for information, not advice: "What are the typical treatments for [condition]?" rather than "Should I get [treatment]?"

Focus on preparation: "Help me prepare questions for my doctor about [topic]" positions AI as a preparation tool.

Be explicit about your intent: "I'm not asking for medical advice, I'm trying to understand the options before my appointment."

Key Takeaways

  • Be specific: include symptoms, timing, and relevant personal details
  • Provide context: age, conditions, medications, and why you're asking
  • Request the format you need: simple language, bullet points, or tables
  • State your goal: what you're trying to accomplish with this information
  • Follow up to clarify or go deeper - it's a conversation, not a search
  • Ask AI about its uncertainty: "How confident are you about this?"
  • Save prompts that work well as templates for future use
  • Always verify important health information with your healthcare team
