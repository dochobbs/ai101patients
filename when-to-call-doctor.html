SAFETY FIRST

When to Trust AI vs. Call Your Doctor

Red flags that need human care. When AI helps, when it doesn't, and when to pick up the phone.

15 min read

Never Use AI for Emergencies

Call 911 or go to the ER immediately if you have:

  • Chest pain or pressure
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Sudden severe headache
  • Signs of stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty)
  • Severe allergic reaction
  • Uncontrolled bleeding
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Thoughts of harming yourself or others
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • High fever (over 103F/39.4C) with confusion or difficulty breathing

AI cannot assess emergencies. Don't waste time typing—call for help.

Why This Decision Matters

Every day, people face a decision: Should I ask AI about this health concern, or should I call my doctor? Getting this decision right can be the difference between appropriate care and delayed treatment—or between peace of mind and unnecessary anxiety.

AI can be a valuable tool for health information, but it has real limitations. Understanding when AI is helpful and when it might lead you astray is one of the most important skills for using these tools safely.

Research Context

Studies show that while AI chatbots can provide helpful health information for general questions, they struggle with urgency assessment. AI often fails to recognize when symptoms indicate a medical emergency, either providing false reassurance or generating unnecessary alarm.

Source: Medical AI assessment studies, 2023-2024

The Stoplight Framework

Not everything is an emergency, but not everything can wait for your next appointment either. Here's a simple framework for knowing when AI is appropriate—and when it's not.

RED — Stop. Call Now.

Don't use AI. Contact a healthcare provider immediately.

  • Any symptom that feels like an emergency to you
  • Sudden changes in condition
  • Symptoms that are getting rapidly worse
  • High fever with other concerning symptoms
  • Mental health crisis
  • New symptoms after starting a medication
  • Your gut says "something is really wrong"
  • Symptoms in a very young child or elderly person
  • Symptoms in someone with a compromised immune system
  • Unusual reactions to treatments

YELLOW — Proceed with Caution

AI might help you prepare, but talk to your doctor soon.

  • Persistent symptoms that aren't improving
  • Questions about your specific diagnosis or treatment
  • Concerns about medication side effects
  • New health issues you haven't discussed with a provider
  • Anything that's worrying you enough to search online
  • Symptoms that have lasted more than a few days
  • Recurring health problems
  • Questions about test results you've received

GREEN — AI Can Help Here

Good situations to use AI as a resource.

  • Understanding medical terms and concepts
  • Preparing questions for an upcoming appointment
  • Learning about a condition you've already been diagnosed with
  • Understanding your lab results (with follow-up from your doctor)
  • General health and wellness questions
  • Organizing your thoughts about symptoms before calling
  • Explaining what a test or procedure involves
  • Learning about preventive health measures

Real-World Scenarios

Let's look at some common situations and how to think through them:

Scenario: New symptom you've never had before

Wrong approach: Ask AI "what could this be?" and accept the answer.

Better approach: Use AI to understand what questions to ask, then contact your doctor. New symptoms need professional evaluation—AI can't examine you.

Scenario: Understanding a diagnosis you just received

Good use of AI: "I was just diagnosed with [condition]. Can you explain what this means in simple terms?"

Caution: Verify any specific treatment information with your doctor, and bring questions to your follow-up appointment.

Scenario: Wondering if a side effect is from your medication

Yellow light: AI can tell you common side effects, but contact your doctor or pharmacist about YOUR experience. Some side effects need immediate attention; others are normal.

Scenario: Chest pain that comes and goes

Red light: Don't ask AI about chest pain. Call your doctor or go to the ER. AI cannot assess cardiac symptoms and could provide false reassurance.

Scenario: Preparing for an appointment about a chronic condition

Good use of AI: "What questions should I ask my doctor about managing diabetes better?" Use AI to prepare, then have the conversation with your doctor.

Learn More: Why AI struggles with urgency

Urgency assessment requires clinical judgment—the ability to integrate multiple signals and recognize patterns that indicate danger. AI processes text, but it can't see that you're pale, hear that you're short of breath, or notice that you're clutching your chest.

Additionally, the same symptom can be benign in one context and life-threatening in another. A headache after a good night's sleep is different from a headache after a head injury. AI often lacks this contextual understanding.

Studies show AI tends to either over-alarm (suggesting everything could be serious) or under-recognize (providing false reassurance about concerning symptoms). Neither is helpful for making good decisions.

Trust Your Gut

If something feels wrong, it probably deserves attention from a real person. AI can't see you. It can't hear the worry in your voice. It can't notice that you look pale or are breathing fast.

Your instincts matter. If you're anxious enough to be searching symptoms at 2 AM, that anxiety itself is information. Don't let AI reassure you out of seeking care you might need.

Many serious conditions are first noticed because the patient "knew something was wrong" even when they couldn't articulate exactly what. That gut feeling is worth honoring.

Pearl

"I'd rather look silly for going in and it's nothing, than stay home and it's something." This is a good philosophy. AI can't make this judgment call for you.

Special Populations: When to Be Extra Careful

Certain groups need extra caution when using AI for health information:

Children

Children often can't describe symptoms accurately, and many conditions present differently in children than adults. AI trained primarily on adult health information may give inappropriate advice for pediatric concerns. When in doubt, call your pediatrician.

Pregnant Women

Pregnancy changes everything—normal ranges, medication safety, symptom interpretation. AI may not adequately account for pregnancy in its responses. Any concerning symptoms during pregnancy warrant a call to your OB or midwife.

Elderly Adults

Older adults often present with atypical symptoms—heart attacks without chest pain, infections without fever. AI may miss these atypical presentations. Caution is especially important for anyone over 65 or with multiple health conditions.

People with Chronic Conditions

If you have diabetes, heart disease, kidney disease, or other chronic conditions, symptoms may mean something different for you than for a healthy person. Your doctor knows your baseline; AI doesn't.

Immunocompromised Individuals

If your immune system is compromised (from medication, treatment, or disease), infections can progress faster and present differently. What would be minor in a healthy person could be serious for you. Err on the side of calling your doctor.

Learn More: Why "normal" doesn't mean "normal for you"

AI typically provides information based on population averages. But your "normal" might be different. A blood pressure of 140/90 might be high for most people but actually good for someone whose pressure is usually higher.

Your doctor tracks your trends over time and knows your baseline. AI only knows what you tell it in the moment. This is why AI-provided "normal ranges" should always be discussed with your doctor in the context of your personal history.

When AI and Your Doctor Disagree

What if AI tells you something different from what your doctor said? Trust your doctor.

Here's why:

  • Your doctor has examined you. AI hasn't.
  • Your doctor knows your full history. AI knows what you've told it.
  • Your doctor can order tests and adjust treatment. AI can only talk.
  • Your doctor is accountable for your care. AI is not.
  • Your doctor made decisions based on factors you might not have shared with AI.

That said, if AI raises a question that concerns you, bring it up with your doctor. "I read that some people with my condition also need X. Is that something I should ask about?" A good doctor will welcome your questions.

Warning

Never stop taking a medication or change your treatment because AI suggested it. Always talk to your prescribing provider first. AI doesn't know why your doctor made the choices they made for your specific situation.

Using AI to Support (Not Replace) Care

The best use of AI is to make your conversations with your healthcare team better:

  • Before appointments: Use AI to generate questions, understand your condition, organize your concerns
  • After appointments: Use AI to clarify things you didn't understand, research terms the doctor used
  • Between appointments: Use AI for general learning about your health, not for diagnosing new problems
  • For preparation: Organize your symptom timeline, list your medications, think through your questions

How to Frame Questions to AI

The way you ask AI questions affects the quality of responses you get:

  • Better: "What questions should I ask my doctor about my diabetes?" (uses AI as preparation tool)
  • Risky: "Do I have diabetes?" (uses AI as diagnostic tool)
  • Better: "Explain what metformin does" (general education)
  • Risky: "Should I take metformin?" (treatment decision)
Try This

After your next doctor's appointment, try asking ChatGPT: "My doctor said I have [condition]. Can you explain what that means in simple terms?" Then bring any follow-up questions to your next visit.

Learn More: The "second opinion" trap

Some people use AI as a "second opinion" on their doctor's advice. This can be problematic for several reasons:

AI doesn't have the context your doctor has. It doesn't know why certain choices were made for you specifically. It might suggest an alternative that sounds reasonable in general but isn't right for your situation.

If you genuinely want a second opinion, get one from another doctor who can actually examine you and review your full records—not from a chatbot.

Learn More: When AI can actually help with urgent decisions

There's one situation where AI might help with urgent decisions: helping you articulate what's happening so you can communicate better with emergency services or a nurse hotline.

For example, if you're unsure how to describe symptoms, AI can help you organize your thoughts: "Help me describe my symptoms clearly to the nurse hotline." But this is about communication, not diagnosis—you're still talking to a real person who makes the actual assessment.

Even in this case, don't delay calling. Call first, then organize your thoughts. Never let AI-assisted preparation delay seeking emergency care.

Quick Decision Guide

Ask yourself these questions:

  1. Is this urgent? If yes → Call your doctor or go to urgent care/ER
  2. Am I thinking about changing my treatment? If yes → Talk to your doctor first
  3. Is this about MY specific health situation? If yes → Your doctor knows you better than AI
  4. Am I just trying to understand something better? If yes → AI can help, but verify important facts
  5. Have I been putting off calling the doctor? If yes → That's probably a sign you should call
  6. Would I be relieved to hear "it's nothing"? If yes → That relief should come from a doctor, not AI
Pearl

When in doubt, call. Most doctor's offices have nurse lines. Many have patient portals where you can message questions. These are real humans who can help you decide if something needs attention now or can wait.

Building a Good Relationship with Your Healthcare Team

The best way to know when to call your doctor is to have a good relationship with your healthcare team. Here's how to build that:

  • Ask about their preferences: "When should I call versus message versus wait for an appointment?"
  • Know your access points: Patient portal, nurse line, after-hours number, urgent care options
  • Don't apologize for calling: That's what they're there for
  • Be honest: Tell them if you've been researching online or using AI—they can help put that information in context
  • Follow up: If something isn't improving, call back—you're not being a bother
Try This

At your next appointment, ask your doctor: "What are your guidelines for when I should call versus wait? What symptoms would you want me to contact you about right away?" Having this conversation in advance makes future decisions easier.

Key Takeaways

  • Never use AI for emergencies—call 911 or go to the ER
  • Use the stoplight framework: Red (call now), Yellow (talk to doctor soon), Green (AI can help)
  • Trust your gut—if something feels wrong, seek real medical attention
  • Special populations (children, pregnant women, elderly, chronically ill) need extra caution
  • When AI and your doctor disagree, trust your doctor
  • Use AI to support your healthcare conversations, not replace them
  • Never change medications based on AI advice without talking to your provider
  • Build a relationship with your healthcare team so you know when to call
