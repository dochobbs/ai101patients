AI Tools You Should Know

The chatbots, search engines, and specialized tools people are using for health questions.

18 min read

The AI Landscape

There are now dozens of AI tools available, and it can be overwhelming to know which to use. This guide focuses on the ones people actually use for health questions—and what each is good (and not good) at.

The AI landscape is evolving rapidly. New tools launch every month, existing tools add new features, and the capabilities keep improving. Rather than trying to track every new release, this module will help you understand the major categories of tools and how to evaluate any AI tool for health use.

Pearl

No single AI tool is "best" for everything. Different tools have different strengths. Knowing what each does well helps you pick the right one for your question.

Learn More: How AI tools differ from each other

Even though many AI chatbots seem similar on the surface, they differ in important ways:

Training data: Different companies train their AI on different data sets. This affects what the AI knows and how it responds.

Safety guardrails: Companies implement different levels of caution around health topics. Some AI tools are more willing to discuss symptoms; others are more cautious.

Web access: Some tools can search the internet for current information; others only know what was in their training data.

Integrations: Tools like ChatGPT Health can connect to your actual medical records, while others can only work with information you type in.

The Big Chatbots

These are the main AI assistants people use for general conversations, including health questions.

ChatGPT (OpenAI)

Free + Paid tiers Web, iOS, Android Most popular

The most widely used AI chatbot with over 200 million weekly active users. Now includes ChatGPT Health, which can connect to your medical records. Good for general health questions, explaining concepts, and preparing for appointments.

Best for: General health questions, understanding medical terms, conversation-style learning

Limitation: Can hallucinate, especially with specific medical facts

Source: OpenAI ChatGPT Health announcement, January 2026

Claude (Anthropic)

Free + Paid tiers Web, iOS, Android

Known for thoughtful, nuanced responses and strong reasoning capabilities. Often better at acknowledging uncertainty and limitations. Good for longer conversations where you want careful reasoning about complex health topics.

Best for: Complex questions requiring nuance, understanding different perspectives, detailed explanations

Limitation: No health records integration (as of early 2026)

Gemini (Google)

Free + Paid tiers Web, iOS, Android

Google's AI, integrated with Google services. Can search the web for current information and connects with your Google account. Good for questions where recent information matters or when you want to quickly verify facts.

Best for: Questions needing current information, integration with Google ecosystem

Limitation: Sometimes pulls unreliable web sources; requires careful source verification

Copilot (Microsoft)

Free + Paid tiers Web, Windows, Edge

Microsoft's AI assistant, integrated with Bing search and Microsoft products. Good for users already in the Microsoft ecosystem. Also available in Bing search results.

Best for: Windows users, integration with Microsoft 365, quick web-verified answers

Limitation: Less commonly used for health questions specifically

Learn More: Free vs. paid tiers - is it worth upgrading?

Most AI chatbots offer both free and paid options. Here's what you typically get with paid subscriptions:

Better models: Paid tiers often give access to more advanced AI models that are more accurate and capable of complex reasoning.

Faster responses: During busy times, free users may experience delays or be temporarily limited. Paid users get priority.

More features: Advanced features like image analysis, longer conversations, and integrations (like ChatGPT Health) may require paid subscriptions.

Higher limits: Free tiers often have limits on how many questions you can ask per day. Paid removes or raises these limits.

For occasional health questions, free tiers are usually sufficient. If you're managing a chronic condition or using AI regularly for health research, paid tiers may be worth it.

AI Search Tools

These tools combine AI with web search, giving you answers with sources.

Perplexity

Free + Paid tiers Web, iOS, Android

AI-powered search that provides answers with cited sources. You can see where the information came from and verify it yourself. Increasingly popular for health research because you can check the sources directly.

Best for: Research questions where you want to verify sources, comparing information from multiple websites

Limitation: Quality depends on source websites it pulls from; may include unreliable sources

Google AI Overviews

Free Google Search

AI-generated summaries that appear at the top of Google search results. You're probably already seeing these. They synthesize information from multiple web sources and provide links to the original pages.

Best for: Quick answers while doing regular web searches

Limitation: Sometimes summarizes unreliable sources; has had notable errors in early rollouts

In the News

2025-2026: AI search has become mainstream. Most people now encounter AI-generated health information whether they're specifically seeking it or not, just by using regular search engines. This makes understanding how to evaluate AI-generated content more important than ever.

Using AI Search for Health Research

When using AI search tools like Perplexity for health questions, follow these practices:

  • Check the sources: Click through to verify the information comes from reputable medical sites
  • Look for medical sources: Prefer answers that cite Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, NIH, CDC, or medical journals
  • Be wary of single sources: The best answers synthesize information from multiple reliable sources
  • Note the date: Medical information can become outdated; check when sources were published
Try This

Try the same health question in both a regular chatbot (like ChatGPT) and an AI search tool (like Perplexity). Notice how Perplexity shows you exactly where the information came from, while ChatGPT doesn't always cite sources. Consider which approach works better for different types of questions.

Specialized Tools

These tools have specific purposes beyond general conversation.

NotebookLM (Google)

Free Web

Upload documents (like your discharge summary or a research article) and ask questions about them. The AI only answers based on what you uploaded—reducing hallucination risk significantly.

Best for: Understanding medical documents you've received, summarizing long articles, getting explanations of complex reports

Limitation: Only knows what you upload to it; cannot draw on broader medical knowledge

Sora 2 (OpenAI)

Paid Web

AI video generation. Less directly relevant to health questions, but increasingly used for creating educational content and explanations.

Best for: Visual learners who want to see concepts explained

Limitation: Generated content may not be medically accurate; should not be used for medical instruction

Nano Banana Pro (Google)

Free + Paid Web

Google's image generation tool. Can create visual explanations of concepts. Use with caution for medical imagery—AI-generated medical images can be misleading or anatomically incorrect.

Best for: Creating simple visual aids or diagrams for personal understanding

Limitation: Don't use for actual medical imagery or diagnosis; may generate inaccurate anatomical representations

Try This

If you have a medical document you're trying to understand (like discharge instructions), try uploading it to NotebookLM and asking: "What are the most important things I need to do based on this document?" Then ask: "Explain the medical terms in this document in simple language."

Learn More: How NotebookLM reduces AI errors

One of the biggest problems with AI is "hallucination" - making up information that sounds plausible but isn't true. NotebookLM addresses this by limiting the AI to only the documents you upload.

When you ask NotebookLM a question, it can only answer based on the text in your uploaded documents. It will tell you if the answer isn't in the documents rather than making something up.

This makes it especially useful for:

  • Understanding your medical records or test results
  • Summarizing long research articles about your condition
  • Finding specific information in discharge instructions
  • Comparing information across multiple documents

The tradeoff is that NotebookLM can't provide general medical context or explain things not mentioned in your documents. For that, you'd use a general chatbot alongside NotebookLM.

Health-Focused AI Tools

These tools are specifically designed for health questions.

Ada Health

Free iOS, Android

AI-powered symptom checker that asks questions about your symptoms and suggests possible conditions. Developed with medical expertise and used by healthcare organizations worldwide.

Best for: Initial symptom assessment before deciding to call your doctor

Limitation: Cannot diagnose—only suggests possibilities to discuss with your doctor

Source: Ada Health has been rated highly for accuracy in peer-reviewed studies comparing symptom checker apps.

K Health

Free + Paid iOS, Android

AI symptom checker that can also connect you with real doctors for telehealth visits. The AI helps gather your information before a human consultation, making appointments more efficient.

Best for: Getting from symptoms to doctor consultation efficiently

Limitation: Full features require payment; not a replacement for your regular doctor

Drugs.com Medication Guide

Free Web, iOS, Android

While not purely AI, this tool uses intelligent features to help you look up medications, check for interactions, and understand side effects. The information is medically reviewed.

Best for: Medication information, interaction checking, pill identification

Limitation: Always verify with your pharmacist for your specific situation

Learn More: Should I use health-specific AI vs. general chatbots?

Health-specific tools like Ada are designed with medical input and often have better safety guardrails. They're usually better for symptom checking.

General chatbots like ChatGPT are more flexible—you can ask anything—but have less medical-specific safety features. They're often better for general learning and preparing questions.

For symptom questions: consider health-specific tools first. For understanding and learning: general chatbots work well. For anything serious: talk to a real healthcare provider.

Learn More: How symptom checker AI works

Symptom checker apps like Ada use a combination of AI and medical knowledge bases. When you describe your symptoms, the AI:

Asks clarifying questions: Unlike general chatbots, symptom checkers follow structured protocols to gather the specific information doctors would want to know.

Considers probability: They rank possible conditions by how likely they are, not just whether they're possible. A headache is more likely to be tension than a brain tumor.

Account for red flags: They're programmed to recognize warning signs that need immediate medical attention and will tell you to seek emergency care.

However, even the best symptom checkers are only as good as the information you provide, and they cannot examine you. They're best used as a first step to help you decide if and how urgently you need to see a doctor.

Emerging Tools to Watch

The AI health landscape is evolving rapidly. Here are some emerging categories and tools to be aware of:

Voice-Based AI Assistants

AI assistants like Amazon Alexa and Google Assistant are adding more health features. You can ask basic health questions, set medication reminders, and in some cases, get health information read aloud. These are convenient but generally less capable than dedicated health AI tools.

AI in Patient Portals

Some healthcare systems are integrating AI into their patient portals. These AI assistants can help you understand your test results, schedule appointments, and prepare for visits—all within your healthcare provider's system.

Mental Health AI

Apps like Woebot and Wysa use AI for mental health support. They're not replacements for therapy but can provide cognitive behavioral techniques, mood tracking, and coping strategies between sessions or while waiting to see a therapist.

Warning

Be cautious with AI tools that make bold health claims or promise to diagnose conditions. Legitimate health AI tools are clear about their limitations and always recommend consulting healthcare providers for diagnosis and treatment.

Questions to Ask When Evaluating Any AI Tool

When you encounter a new AI tool for health, ask yourself:

  • Who made it? Is it from a reputable company? Do they have medical advisors?
  • What are its limitations? Legitimate tools are clear about what they can't do.
  • Where does the information come from? Can you verify the sources?
  • How is my data protected? Read the privacy policy, especially for health data.
  • Does it replace or complement healthcare? Be wary of tools claiming to replace doctors.

Key Takeaways

  • Different AI tools have different strengths—match the tool to your question
  • ChatGPT is most popular and now offers health records integration through ChatGPT Health
  • Perplexity and similar tools let you verify sources, which is valuable for health research
  • NotebookLM is great for understanding documents you upload and reduces hallucination risk
  • Health-specific apps like Ada are designed for symptom checking with medical oversight
  • AI search is now built into regular search engines—you're likely already encountering it
  • No AI tool replaces your doctor—they're research assistants, not healthcare providers
  • When evaluating new tools, check who made them, their limitations, and privacy policies
